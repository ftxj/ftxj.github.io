The Chameleon's Limit: Investigating Persona Collapse and Homogenization in Large Language Models

April 28, 2026 6-minute read
Paper-Digest • 2026-04-28

arXiv: 2604.24698 · PDF

Authors: Yunze Xiao, Vivienne J. Zhang, Chenghao Yang, Ningshan Ma, Weihao Xuan, Jen-tse Huang

Affiliations: CMU, UChicago, MIT, 2077.ai, UTokyo, RIKEN AIP, JHU

Primary category: cs.CL · all: cs.CL

Matched keywords: large language model, llm, agent, multi-agent, rag, reasoning

TL;DR

The paper identifies “Persona Collapse” — LLM agents with distinct profiles converge to a narrow behavioral mode — and proposes a Coverage/Uniformity/Complexity framework. Across 10 LLMs on BFI-44, moral reasoning, and self-introduction, higher per-persona fidelity counter-intuitively yields the most stereotyped populations.

Motivation

LLM-based multi-agent simulations (social science, market research, RLHF preference modeling) depend on populations of agents behaving diversely, not merely individually plausibly. The authors document that despite assigning agents distinct profiles, outputs collapse into a homogeneous mode — a failure mode they name Persona Collapse. Today’s practitioners running Qwen3-32B, GPT-class, or Llama-class agents for population simulation typically check per-persona fidelity (does this agent sound like its profile?) and declare success, missing that the population as a whole is degenerate. No prior framework simultaneously measures how much of the persona space is filled, how evenly, and how richly. The gap matters now because downstream uses — synthetic survey data, agent societies, moral-judgment benchmarks — treat LLM populations as proxies for human populations, and collapsed populations silently bias every conclusion drawn from them. The authors argue the problem has been invisible because evaluation has been single-agent, and that population-level diagnostics are needed to expose it.

Key Ideas

  • Name and formalize Persona Collapse: agents with distinct profiles converge to a narrow behavioral mode.
  • Three complementary population metrics: Coverage, Uniformity, Complexity.
  • Collapse is two-axis: along dimensions (diverse on one axis, degenerate on another) and domains (same model collapses in personality yet spreads in moral reasoning).
  • Behavioral variation tracks coarse demographic stereotypes, not fine-grained individual profile specifics.
  • Counter-intuitive finding: models with the highest per-persona fidelity produce the most stereotyped populations — individual plausibility trades off against population diversity.

Figure 1 grounds the Persona Collapse claim: two personas differing across multiple identity dimensions both receive the same neutral response from Qwen3-32B on a socially sensitive judgment task, and at the population level the most conservative and most liberal persona pools yield indistinguishable aggregate behavior. It supports Key Idea 1 concretely — collapse is not a subtle statistical artifact but visible at the single-prompt level, which is why per-persona evaluation misses it.

Method

A three-metric population diagnostic framework. Coverage measures the fraction of the persona/behavior space the population occupies. Uniformity measures how evenly agents spread across that occupied space (clumped vs. well-distributed). Complexity is operationalized via Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) to capture richness of behavioral patterns. These are applied both at the dimension level (per-axis of the profile) and domain level (across tasks). Item-level diagnostics additionally compare per-item variance against demographic stereotype baselines to test whether variation is individual or stereotype-driven. Per-persona fidelity (ρ) and trait polarization are tracked to surface the fidelity-vs-diversity trade-off.

Experiments

  • Models: 10 LLMs, including Qwen3-32B.
  • Tasks / domains: personality simulation (BFI-44), moral reasoning, self-introduction.
  • Scale: 1,144 personas per model on BFI-44.
  • Metrics: Coverage, Uniformity, Complexity (LID), persona fidelity ρ, trait polarization, item-level stereotype correlation.
  • Reference: human population baseline for BFI-44.

Results

No model simultaneously achieves high coverage and high complexity; the human reference sits in the upper-right that all models miss. Collapse patterns differ by model and by domain — a model may be the most collapsed on personality yet the most diverse on moral reasoning. Item-level variation aligns with coarse demographic stereotypes rather than the fine-grained persona specification. Highest per-persona fidelity ↔ most stereotyped populations.

Figure 2 (Coverage subplot) backs the “no model fills the persona space” claim from Results: it shows per-model coverage values, with all LLMs falling well short of the human reference. It isolates the first axis of the Coverage/Uniformity/Complexity framework introduced in Method.

Figure 3 (Uniformity subplot) supports the Uniformity dimension of the framework, showing how evenly — or unevenly — each model’s agents spread across the occupied region. Combined with Coverage, it distinguishes “small but even” collapse from “wide but clumpy” behavior, i.e. the two-axis collapse claim.

Figure 4 (Complexity / LID subplot) backs the Complexity metric: it quantifies behavioral richness per model and shows that several models achieving respectable coverage nonetheless have low LID — they spread wide but shallow. This is the concrete evidence that coverage alone overstates diversity.

Figure 5 is the headline diagnostic plot on BFI-44 across all 10 models and 1,144 personas. Left panel (Coverage vs. Complexity/LID) places the human reference in the upper-right while every LLM falls short on at least one axis — directly supporting “no model simultaneously achieves high coverage and high complexity.” Right panel (persona fidelity ρ vs. trait polarization) shows the counter-intuitive headline: higher ρ correlates with more polarization/stereotyping, evidencing the fidelity-vs-diversity trade-off.

Conclusion

Practitioners using LLM populations for simulation, synthetic data, or preference modeling should stop treating per-persona fidelity as evidence of population realism — the paper’s headline finding is that these quantities trade off, with the highest-fidelity models producing the most stereotyped crowds. The Coverage/Uniformity/Complexity toolkit is the concrete contribution to adopt. Honest limits: results rest on three tasks (BFI-44, moral reasoning, self-introduction) with a fixed 1,144-persona pool, LID as the complexity proxy is one choice among many, and the abstract does not report whether prompting strategies (temperature, system-prompt engineering) mitigate collapse.

Novelty Check

  • Prior art on LLM homogenization: Park et al.’s Generative Agents and follow-ups noted behavioral convergence anecdotally; this paper’s novelty is making it quantitative at population scale with three decomposed metrics.
  • Prior art on diversity metrics: Coverage/diversity scoring for generated text (Self-BLEU, distinct-n, LID-based complexity) exists; the new contribution is applying them jointly at persona-population level and tying the result to fidelity.
  • Prior art on demographic stereotyping in LLMs: BBQ, BOLD, and persona-bias studies document stereotyping per group; new here is the individual-vs-stereotype variance decomposition and the fidelity↔stereotyping trade-off.

Verdict: genuinely new framing and the fidelity-vs-diversity trade-off is a non-obvious empirical finding; the individual metric ingredients are not new, but their joint population-level use is.

Open Questions

  • Do decoding-time interventions (temperature, nucleus, persona-conditioned fine-tuning) actually move models toward the human upper-right?
  • Is collapse driven by RLHF alignment, pretraining prior, or prompt format — which is dominant?
  • How do results change with larger persona pools, richer profile schemas, or non-Western demographics?
  • Does the fidelity↔stereotyping trade-off hold for open-ended agentic tasks beyond survey-style items?

Original abstract

Applications based on large language models (LLMs), such as multi-agent simulations, require population diversity among agents. We identify a pervasive failure mode we term \emph{Persona Collapse}: agents each assigned a distinct profile nonetheless converge into a narrow behavioral mode, producing a homogeneous simulated population. To quantify persona collapse, we propose a framework that measures how much of the persona space a population occupies (Coverage), how evenly agents spread across it (Uniformity), and how rich the resulting behavioral patterns are (Complexity). Evaluating ten LLMs on personality simulation (BFI-44), moral reasoning, and self-introduction, we observe persona collapse along two axes: (1) Dimensions: a model can appear diverse on one axis yet structurally degenerate on another, and (2) Domains: the same model may collapse the most in personality yet be the most diverse in moral reasoning. Furthermore, item-level diagnostics reveal that behavioral variation tracks coarse demographic stereotypes rather than the fine-grained individual differences specified in each persona. Counter-intuitively, \textbf{the models achieving the highest per-persona fidelity consistently produce the most stereotyped populations}. We release our toolkit and data to support population-level evaluation of LLMs.
