DepthKV: Layer-Dependent KV Cache Pruning for Long-Context LLM Inference

April 28, 2026 6-minute read
Paper-Digest • 2026-04-28

arXiv: 2604.24647 · PDF

Authors: Zahra Dehghanighobadi, Asja Fischer

Affiliations: Ruhr University Bochum, UAR Research Center for Trustworthy Data Science and Security

Primary category: cs.CL · all: cs.AI, cs.CL

Matched keywords: large language model, llm, reasoning, inference, kv cache, attention

TL;DR

DepthKV replaces uniform KV cache pruning with a layer-dependent allocation that spends a fixed global KV budget where layers are most sensitive, consistently beating uniform pruning at the same global ratio on long-context LLM inference.

Motivation

Long-context LLM serving is bottlenecked by the KV cache, whose memory grows linearly with sequence length. Operators running long-document QA, summarization, or code generation on vLLM/SGLang-style stacks already lean on KV pruning — dropping tokens with low attention scores — to keep inference feasible. The pain: nearly all deployed pruning schemes (H2O, SnapKV, and similar attention-score heuristics) apply the same pruning ratio at every transformer layer, implicitly treating layers as interchangeable consumers of the KV budget. The authors argue — and show empirically — that this is wrong: layers differ sharply in how much accuracy collapses when their KV is pruned, so a uniform policy overspends on insensitive layers and starves critical ones. The gap versus current practice is not a new scoring function for which token to drop; it is a missing budget allocator across layers given a fixed global budget. Nobody had quantified per-layer sensitivity systematically enough to drive that allocation, partly because measuring it requires probing each layer independently. DepthKV’s claim is narrow but concrete: keep your favorite token-level pruning criterion, just stop allocating the budget uniformly.

Key Ideas

  • Uniform per-layer KV pruning is suboptimal; sensitivity to pruning is highly layer-dependent with sharp drops concentrated at specific layers.
  • Per-layer sensitivity can be measured by single-layer pruning experiments using ROUGE-1 and a YapScore metric.
  • Post-attention InfoNCE correlates negatively with performance drop, giving a cheap proxy for layer importance.
  • DepthKV allocates a fixed global KV budget across layers proportional to sensitivity, preserving more tokens in critical layers.

Figure 2 is the empirical backbone of the “layers aren’t equal” claim. It pruning each layer’s KV cache in isolation and plotting z-scored ROUGE-1 per layer; markers flag the worst-hit layer per dataset. As the body states, “the impact of pruning is highly layer-dependent, with sharp performance drops concentrated at specific layers” — directly motivating a non-uniform allocator.

Figure 3 replicates the sensitivity story using YapScore (z-normalized per dataset) under single-layer prefill-stage pruning. Each curve is a dataset, and the shapes agree with Figure 2: a handful of layers dominate the degradation regardless of metric, reinforcing that ROUGE-1 isn’t an artifact and that per-layer importance is a real, measurable signal DepthKV can exploit.

Figure 4 links the sensitivity signal to a cheap proxy: standardized post-attention InfoNCE versus ROUGE-1 drop across layers on arXiv. The body reports a “consistent negative correlation between InfoNCE and performance degradation” — layers with lower InfoNCE suffer more when pruned — which is what makes sensitivity-guided allocation practical without exhaustive per-layer ablations at serving time.

Method

DepthKV is a layer-dependent KV pruning framework. Given a fixed global KV budget (same total tokens as uniform pruning), it redistributes the budget across transformer layers based on a sensitivity score per layer, derived from single-layer pruning experiments and correlated with post-attention InfoNCE as a cheap proxy. Critical (high-sensitivity) layers retain more tokens; insensitive layers are pruned more aggressively. The token-level selection criterion (attention-score-based eviction) is orthogonal and inherited from existing methods — DepthKV changes how much budget each layer gets, not which token within a layer gets dropped.

Figure 1 contrasts the two allocation regimes that define the paper’s contribution. On the left, uniform allocation assigns an identical KV budget to every transformer layer; on the right, DepthKV reallocates that same total budget so sensitive layers (highlighted) keep more tokens while insensitive layers are pruned harder. This is the exact mechanism Method describes — a budget allocator sitting on top of any standard token-eviction policy.

Experiments

Evaluation spans multiple LLMs and long-context tasks. Sensitivity probes use ROUGE-1 and YapScore on datasets including arXiv. Downstream evaluation includes the GSM-∞ mathematical reasoning benchmark. The baseline is uniform-ratio KV pruning at the same global pruning ratio; metrics are task accuracy / generation quality.

Results

DepthKV consistently outperforms uniform pruning at matched global pruning ratios across models and tasks. On GSM-∞, all DepthKV variants beat uniform pruning on mathematical reasoning.

Figure 5 shows GSM-∞ accuracy across KV pruning settings. The body states that on mathematical reasoning “all variants outperforming uniform pruning on GSM-∞,” which is the headline result: at identical total KV budget, redistributing across layers recovers accuracy uniform pruning throws away. The figure supports the claim that sensitivity-driven allocation is strictly better utilization of the cache, not just a different trade-off.

Conclusion

The practitioner takeaway: if you already run KV pruning in production, switching from a uniform per-layer ratio to a sensitivity-weighted allocation is a near-free accuracy win at the same memory budget, and post-attention InfoNCE gives a cheap way to estimate the weights. The strongest supporting evidence is that all DepthKV variants beat uniform pruning on GSM-∞ at matched global ratio. Honest limits: the abstract reports no latency/throughput numbers, no extreme-ratio stress test, and no comparison against non-uniform schemes that allocate by attention entropy or head importance rather than layer sensitivity — so the gain over non-uniform baselines is unquantified here.

Novelty Check

  • PyramidKV / PyramidInfer — already argue for non-uniform KV allocation across layers using a pyramid-shaped budget. DepthKV’s difference is a data-driven sensitivity allocator tied to single-layer pruning ROUGE/YapScore and InfoNCE, rather than a fixed pyramid prior.
  • H2O / SnapKV — token-level eviction with uniform per-layer ratio. DepthKV is orthogonal: it keeps their token scoring and changes the layer budget split.
  • Ada-KV — adaptive per-head budget allocation. Similar spirit, different granularity (head vs. layer).

Verdict: incremental but real. The non-uniform-across-layers idea is not new (PyramidKV predates this), but grounding the allocation in measured per-layer sensitivity + an InfoNCE proxy is a legitimate contribution. Risk of being “old wine”: medium — the framing as if uniform-across-layers were standard ignores pyramid-style prior art.

Open Questions

  • How does DepthKV compare head-to-head against PyramidKV / Ada-KV, not just uniform pruning?
  • Latency and memory overhead of computing InfoNCE-based allocation at serve time?
  • Does the sensitivity profile transfer across model families, or must it be re-profiled per checkpoint?
  • Behavior at aggressive global ratios (e.g. 10–20%) where uniform pruning collapses?

Original abstract

Long-context reasoning is a critical capability of large language models (LLMs), enabling applications such as long-document understanding, summarization, and code generation. However, efficient autoregressive inference relies on the key-value (KV) cache, whose memory footprint grows linearly with sequence length, leading to a major memory bottleneck. To mitigate this overhead, KV cache pruning methods discard cached tokens with low attention scores during inference. Most existing methods apply a uniform pruning ratio across layers, implicitly assuming that all layers contribute equally to overall model performance. We show that this assumption is suboptimal, as layers differ significantly in their sensitivity to pruning. We propose DepthKV, a layer-dependent pruning framework that allocates a fixed global KV budget across layers based on their sensitivity, rather than using a uniform allocation. Across multiple models and tasks, DepthKV consistently outperforms uniform pruning at the same global pruning ratio, demonstrating more effective utilization of the KV cache budget through layer-dependent allocation.
