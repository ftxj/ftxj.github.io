FlashOverlap: Minimizing Tail Latency in Communication Overlap for Distributed LLM Training

April 28, 2026 7-minute read
Paper-Digest • 2026-04-28

arXiv: 2604.24013 · PDF

Authors: Rezaul Karim, Austin Wen, Wang Zongzuo, Weiwei Zhang, Yang Liu, Walid Ahmed

Affiliations: Toronto Ascend Team, Huawei

Primary category: cs.LG · all: cs.CV, cs.DC, cs.LG

Matched keywords: large language model, llm, inference, distributed training, parallelism, gpu, throughput, latency

TL;DR

FlashOverlap replaces collective reduce-scatter and all-gather with decomposed peer-to-peer communication interleaved with partitioned compute, eliminating the tail-latency step that plagues existing slicing-based overlap schemes in distributed LLM training and inference across TP, TPSP, UP, and DP.

Motivation

As LLMs scale past single-device memory, operators rely on tensor parallelism (TP), sequence parallelism (TPSP), ulysses/unified parallelism (UP), and data parallelism (DP), all of which pay a heavy collective-communication tax through ring-based all-gather (AG) and reduce-scatter (RS). In practice, vLLM/SGLang inference serving and Megatron/DeepSpeed training teams currently hide part of this cost by slicing the GEMM into chunks and overlapping each chunk’s compute with the previous chunk’s communication. The gap the authors attack is concrete: existing slicing-based overlap schemes still leave a non-overlappable tail step — the last communication chunk has no remaining compute to hide behind, so its latency is exposed on the critical path. That tail dominates at the small-chunk limit where overlap would otherwise be tightest, and it grows with rank count because ring AG/RS scale as N−1 P2P hops. The authors argue this is not a tuning problem but an algorithmic one: collective AG/RS semantics force a synchronization boundary that any pure data-slicing wrapper inherits. Their bet is that decomposing the collective into explicit P2P steps and co-scheduling the matching compute slices gives an exact schedule whose last step still overlaps, rather than a heuristic that merely shrinks the tail. The scope claim is narrow and honest — TP/TPSP/UP/DP for LLM train and inference — not a general collective replacement.

Key Ideas

  • Identify tail latency of the final communication chunk as the binding constraint in existing overlap methods, not bandwidth.
  • Replace ring AG/RS with decomposed asynchronous P2P send/recv steps that expose every hop to the scheduler.
  • Co-partition the GEMM/attention compute along the matching axis so every P2P step has a compute peer to hide behind, including the last.
  • Provide an exact (not approximate) reordering that preserves AG/RS semantics while removing the exposed tail.
  • One mechanism covers TP, TPSP, UP, and DP — same P2P+partitioned-compute template, different slicing axis.

Figure 7a shows the baseline the paper is attacking: standard attention under TP with a non-overlapped reduce-scatter at the end of the block. The RS bar sits on the critical path after the attention compute finishes, so its latency is fully exposed — this is exactly the “tail” cost that slicing-based overlap schemes only partially shrink. It motivates why an exact P2P-decomposed schedule, not a finer data split, is needed.

Method

FlashOverlap rewrites AG and RS as a sequence of N−1 ring-ordered P2P send/recv pairs and interleaves them with partitioned matmul / attention computations that consume or produce the matching shard. Control flow: at ring step k, rank r launches an async p2p_send of its current shard and an async p2p_recv of the neighbor’s shard while simultaneously running the compute on the shard it already holds; the output slices O_s^r are concatenated at the end. Because every P2P hop — including the last — is paired with a compute slice, no step is left naked on the critical path. The same template instantiates Flash-All-Gather (pre-GEMM, gathers activations) and Flash-Reduce-Scatter (post-GEMM, scatters partial sums). For attention, a Query-Split variant partitions along the Q axis so Flash-RS overlaps with per-head attention compute under TP.

Figure 2 defines the All-Gather baseline the method replaces: every rank starts with its own shard and ends holding the full tensor via a ring pattern. This is the collective whose N−1-step structure FlashOverlap decomposes into independently schedulable P2P hops in Flash-AG.

Figure 3 is the dual, showing Reduce-Scatter: ranks start with full tensors, exchange along the ring, and end holding reduced shards. Flash-RS targets exactly this pattern — the last reduction step is the tail that existing overlap schemes cannot hide.

Figure 4 makes the ring implementation of AG concrete on four ranks, exposing the N−1 = 3 sequential P2P hops that any slicing-based overlap must contend with. FlashOverlap keeps this ring ordering but turns each hop into an explicit schedulable unit.

Figure 5 does the same for Reduce-Scatter on four ranks, again highlighting the three hops and the final reduction step — the one that produces the exposed tail in conventional overlap.

Figure 6 shows the Flash-All-Gather schedule on four ranks: each P2P step is paired with a partial-output computation on the shard already resident, so compute and communication progress together across all ring hops rather than leaving the last hop unpaired. This is the core mechanism behind the TL;DR’s “fine-grained overlap” claim.

Figure 7 is the Flash-Reduce-Scatter counterpart, interleaving async P2P send/recv with partial computations so the reduction’s final step also has compute to hide behind — the concrete step that removes the tail.

Figure 7b (Query-Split Flash-RS) shows the attention-specific instantiation: Q is split along heads, async P2P carries partial outputs between ranks, and per-head attention compute runs concurrently — directly eliminating the exposed RS bar visible in the 7a baseline.

Experiments

The abstract reports experimental evaluation on distributed LLM training and inference under TP, TPSP, UP, and DP, comparing against state-of-the-art slicing-based overlap methods. Reported metrics are end-to-end latency, Model FLOPS Utilization (MFU), and throughput. Specific models, rank counts, hardware, and baseline names are not enumerated in the abstract.

Results

The abstract claims consistently lower latency, higher MFU, and higher throughput than existing overlap techniques, attributed to removing the tail step. No concrete speedup ratios, rank-scaling curves, or MFU percentages are given in the abstract.

Conclusion

The practitioner takeaway is narrow and useful: if you already use chunked overlap for AG/RS in TP/TPSP/UP/DP, the residual cost you still see is mostly the unpaired last step, and that step can be removed by switching to a P2P-decomposed schedule with compute co-partitioned to match. The supporting evidence is the claimed consistent reduction in latency with higher MFU and throughput across parallelism modes. The honest limit: the abstract names no concrete speedup numbers, hardware, or model sizes, so scale of the win is not verifiable from this text alone, and correctness relies on the authors’ “exact algorithm” claim.

Novelty Check

  • CoCoNet / T3 / Centauri: fused compute-communication for collectives — similar goal, but they restructure kernels rather than decompose into scheduler-visible P2P with matched compute slices.
  • Megatron-LM / Flux sequence-parallel overlap: slicing-based AG/RS overlap — the exact baseline class whose tail step this work attacks; the new piece is the exact P2P schedule that pairs the final hop.
  • Ring-Attention / Ulysses: ring P2P with attention compute — same hardware primitive, but applied to sequence-sharded attention, not as a drop-in AG/RS replacement across TP/DP.

Net: genuinely new as a tail-elimination framing with an exact schedule unifying AG and RS across four parallelism modes; the underlying ring-P2P-with-overlap idea is not new on its own.

Open Questions

  • Quantitative gap vs. CoCoNet / T3 / Flux at 8, 16, 64 ranks?
  • Behavior under NVLink vs. RDMA, and when P2P latency (not bandwidth) dominates?
  • Interaction with pipeline parallelism and with expert-parallel MoE all-to-all?
  • Does the “exact” guarantee hold with mixed-precision accumulation order in RS?
  • Memory overhead from holding partial outputs across N−1 steps.

Original abstract

The rapid growth in the size of large language models has necessitated the partitioning of computational workloads across accelerators such as GPUs, TPUs, and NPUs. However, these parallelization strategies incur substantial data communication overhead significantly hindering computational efficiency. While communication-computation overlap presents a promising direction, existing data slicing based solutions suffer from tail latency. To overcome this limitation, this research introduces a novel communication-computation overlap technique to eliminate this tail latency in state of the art overlap methods for distributed LLM training. The aim of this technique is to effectively mitigate communication bottleneck of tensor parallelism and data parallelism for distributed training and inference. In particular, we propose a novel method termed Flash-Overlap that replaces conventional collective operations of reduce-scatter and all-gather with decomposed peer-to-peer (P2P) communication and schedules partitioned computations to enable fine-grained overlap. Our method provides an exact algorithm for reducing communication overhead that eliminates tail latency. Moreover, it presents a versatile solution compatible with data-parallel training and various tensor-level parallelism strategies, including TPSP and UP. Experimental evaluations demonstrate that our technique consistently achieves lower latency, superior Model FLOPS Utilization (MFU), and high throughput.
