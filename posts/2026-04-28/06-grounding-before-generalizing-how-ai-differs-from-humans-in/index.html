Grounding Before Generalizing: How AI Differs from Humans in Causal Transfer

April 28, 2026 7-minute read
Paper-Digest • 2026-04-28

arXiv: 2604.24062 · PDF

Authors: Liangru Xiang, Yuxi Ma, Zhihao Cao, Yixin Zhu, Song-Chun Zhu

Affiliations: Tsinghua University, Peking University, State Key Laboratory of General Artificial Intelligence

Primary category: cs.AI · all: cs.AI

Matched keywords: large language model, llm, agent, rag, reasoning

TL;DR

Using the OpenLock sequential-discovery paradigm, the authors show that LLMs and VLMs only transfer causal structure after an initial “environmental grounding” phase, while humans reuse abstract Common Cause (CC) / Common Effect (CE) schemas from the first attempt. Vision actually degrades performance, exposing a reliance on symbolic, not multimodal, reasoning.

Motivation

Abstract causal transfer — inducing a latent structure in one setting and redeploying it immediately in another — is a core feature of human cognition, and classical RL agents have long been known to fail at it catastrophically. The recent wave of LLMs and VLMs has scrambled the picture: these models score well on static reasoning benchmarks, yet nobody has characterised whether their strength extends to interactive causal learning, where structure must be discovered through sequential exploration and then ported across contexts. For practitioners building agent runtimes, cognitive-science evaluation suites, or any system that claims “reasoning”, this gap matters: if a frontier model only appears to transfer because it re-solves each new environment from scratch, its sample efficiency on open-ended tasks is being overstated. Prior work on OpenLock established humans-vs-RL baselines but predates modern foundation models, and most LLM causal benchmarks are one-shot QA rather than embodied, multi-trial discovery. The authors argue this is the constraint that changes the game — a paradigm designed for humans (CC and CE lock structures, swappable surface features) can finally be run against GPT-/Claude-class models in text, vision, and multimodal conditions. Their framing is narrow and honest: this is about structural transfer under minimal exposure, not causal reasoning writ large.

Key Ideas

  • Reuse OpenLock’s CC / CE sequential-discovery paradigm to probe interactive causal induction in LLMs/VLMs, not just static QA.
  • Introduce environmental grounding as the diagnostic: a phase where models must build environment-specific mappings before any transfer benefit appears.
  • Run matched Text (T), Image (I), and Text+Image (TI) conditions to separate symbolic from multimodal reasoning.
  • Quantify CC vs CE asymmetries as a marker of heuristic bias rather than direction-neutral abstraction.
  • Baseline vs Transfer experiment design isolates structural reuse from per-environment search.

Method

The OpenLock virtual environment contains seven levers and one door; agents must discover a sequential unlocking policy whose underlying causal graph is either Common Cause (CC) or Common Effect (CE). Surface features (lever positions, colours, labels) are re-randomised across environments while the latent structure is held fixed, so any efficiency gain across environments must come from abstracting the graph. Each model is evaluated in three input conditions — text-only (T), image-only (I), and text+image (TI) — against human participants. Experiment 1 (Baseline) measures per-environment discovery cost; Experiment 2 (Transfer) replays the same structure in a fresh environment to measure reuse. The primary metric is marginal discovery cost — attempts needed to find each successive solution — plus an efficiency-gain ratio (Attempts_base − Attempts_trans)/Attempts_base. No new training recipe; frontier models (incl. Claude-4.5) are prompted zero-shot.

Experiments

Environments: OpenLock CC and CE structures. Subjects: human participants and state-of-the-art LLMs/VLMs, with Claude-4.5 called out. Conditions: T, I, TI. Designs: Experiment 1 Baseline vs Experiment 2 Transfer. Metrics: marginal discovery cost per solution index, average attempt count, and transfer efficiency-gain ratio.

Figure 1 backs the paradigm claim in Method: it shows the OpenLock layout (seven levers, one door) on the left and the CC vs CE dependency graphs on the right, illustrating that surface features are swapped across environments while the latent causal structure is the invariant the agent must extract. This is what makes “transfer” measurable rather than confounded with memorisation.

Results

Headline findings:

  • Text-only models match or exceed humans on baseline discovery efficiency.
  • Vision hurts: image-only and text+image conditions overall degrade performance vs text-only, contradicting the “multimodal helps reasoning” narrative for this task.
  • Delayed or absent transfer: even the best models need an environmental-grounding phase before efficiency gains appear; humans benefit from attempt #1.
  • CC/CE asymmetries show up systematically in models but not in humans — evidence of heuristic bias, not direction-neutral abstraction.

Figure 2 supports the “text-only matches humans at baseline” finding: it plots marginal discovery cost (attempts per successive solution) within a single environment, split into CC (left) and CE (right). Claude-4.5 and other models evaluated in the T condition track the human curves closely here — the gap to humans only opens once we ask about cross-environment transfer.

Figure 3 is the central evidence for grounding-dependent transfer. It reports the efficiency-gain ratio (Attempts_base − Attempts_trans)/Attempts_base between Experiment 1 and Experiment 2. Humans show a large positive ratio (immediate reuse of CC/CE structure), whereas models show markedly smaller, delayed, or absent gains — i.e. the transfer benefit that defines abstract causal schema use is muted in LLMs/VLMs.

Figure 4 decomposes that same result solution-by-solution: marginal discovery cost under Baseline (grey) vs Transfer (red) for each model and for humans. For humans the red curve drops from the very first solution; for models the two curves overlap early and only diverge later, which is the concrete signature of “environmental grounding must happen first” that the paper names as its core limitation.

Conclusion

The practitioner takeaway is sharp: current frontier LLMs/VLMs do not carry decontextualised causal schemas across environments — they re-ground, then (sometimes) reuse. The cleanest number supporting this is the muted Experiment 1 → Experiment 2 efficiency-gain ratio versus humans (Figure 3), combined with systematic CC/CE asymmetries that humans don’t exhibit. Honest limits: a single paradigm (OpenLock), a small set of structures (CC and CE only), zero-shot prompting without tool use or scratchpad search strategies, and no fine-tuned or agentic baselines — so the claim is “no emergent human-like transfer”, not “impossible with any training recipe”.

Novelty Check

  • OpenLock with RL agents (Edmonds et al.) — same paradigm and CC/CE structures; the novelty here is swapping in LLMs/VLMs and adding I/TI conditions, not a new environment.
  • CausalBench / static causal-reasoning QA for LLMs — tests causal knowledge one-shot; this paper’s contribution is the interactive, sequential setting and the grounding-vs-transfer decomposition.
  • Analogical-reasoning / ARC-style evaluations — also probe abstraction transfer but without explicit causal graphs; naming environmental grounding as the bottleneck and quantifying CC/CE asymmetry is the genuinely new conceptual contribution. Overall: mostly new diagnosis on an existing paradigm rather than a new method — valuable framing, not a modelling advance.

Open Questions

  • Would chain-of-thought, tool use, or an agentic scratchpad collapse the grounding phase?
  • Does fine-tuning on a handful of CC/CE rollouts induce human-like first-attempt transfer, or does grounding re-emerge on unseen structures?
  • Why does vision degrade performance — tokenizer/perception bottleneck, or genuine failure to fuse modalities for structural inference?
  • Do the CC/CE asymmetries track a specific heuristic (e.g. preference for conjunctive over disjunctive plans) that could be isolated and patched?

Original abstract

Extracting abstract causal structures and applying them to novel situations is a hallmark of human intelligence. While Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Language Models (VLMs) have shown strong performance on a wide range of reasoning tasks, their capacity for interactive causal learning – inducing latent structures through sequential exploration and transferring them across contexts – remains uncharacterized. Human learners accomplish such transfer after minimal exposure, whereas classical Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents fail catastrophically. Whether state-of-the-art Artificial Intelligence (AI) models possess human-like mechanisms for abstract causal structure transfer is an open question. Using the OpenLock paradigm requiring sequential discovery of Common Cause (CC) and Common Effect (CE) structures, here we show that models exhibit fundamentally delayed or absent transfer: even successful models require initial environmental-specific mapping – what we term environmental grounding – before efficiency gains emerge, whereas humans leverage prior structural knowledge from the very first solution attempt. In the text-only condition, models matched or exceeded human discovery efficiency. In contrast, visual information – in both the image-only and text-and-image conditions – overall degraded rather than enhanced performance, revealing a broad reliance on symbolic processing rather than integrated multimodal reasoning. Models further exhibited systematic CC/CE asymmetries absent in humans, suggesting heuristic biases rather than direction-neutral causal abstraction. These findings reveal that large-scale statistical learning does not produce the decontextualized causal schemas underpinning human analogical reasoning, establishing grounding-dependent transfer as a fundamental limitation of current LLMs and VLMs.
